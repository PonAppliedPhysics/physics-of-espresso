Abstract

Espresso preparation is a complex physical process involving fluid mechanics, heat transfer, porous media flow, diffusion, chemical extraction, and colloidal foam formation. This paper summarizes the major physical principles that govern espresso extraction, including pressure-driven flow through a packed coffee bed, particle-size effects, extraction yield, temperature dependence, and crema formation.

01

Introduction

Espresso is produced by forcing hot water through a compacted bed of finely ground coffee. Although it appears simple, the process depends on several coupled physical systems: the grinder, the coffee puck, the espresso machine, and the extraction chemistry.

A typical espresso shot uses water near \( T \approx 90\text{–}96\,^\circ\text{C} \) and pressure near \( P \approx 9 \;\text{bar} \). The final drink depends on grind size, dose, puck geometry, water composition, pressure, temperature, and extraction time.

02

Brew Ratio & Extraction Yield

The brew ratio compares the mass of liquid espresso produced to the dry coffee dose:

\[ R = \frac{m_{\text{beverage}}}{m_{\text{coffee}}} \]

The extraction yield is the fraction of dry coffee dissolved into the beverage:

\[ EY = \frac{m_{\text{dissolved solids}}}{m_{\text{coffee}}} \]

If the beverage has total dissolved solids \( TDS \), then \( m_{\text{dissolved solids}} = TDS \cdot m_{\text{beverage}} \), so:

\[ EY = \frac{TDS \cdot m_{\text{beverage}}}{m_{\text{coffee}}} \]
Worked Example

If \( m_{\text{coffee}} = 18\,\text{g} \), \( m_{\text{beverage}} = 36\,\text{g} \), and \( TDS = 0.10 \), then:

\[ EY = \frac{0.10 \times 36}{18} = 0.20 = 20\% \]
03

Grind Size & Surface Area

Grinding increases the surface area of coffee particles, allowing soluble compounds to dissolve more quickly. Smaller particles generally increase extraction rate, but they also increase resistance to flow.

For a simplified spherical coffee particle with radius \( r \):

\[ A = 4\pi r^2, \qquad V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \]

The surface-area-to-volume ratio is:

\[ \frac{A}{V} = \frac{3}{r} \]

Therefore, as particle radius decreases, the available surface area per unit volume increases.

04

Flow Through the Coffee Puck

The espresso puck can be modeled as a porous medium. Water flow through the puck is often approximated using Darcy's law:

\[ Q = \frac{k\,A}{\mu\,L}\,\Delta P \]
\(Q\) — volumetric flow rate
\(k\) — permeability of the coffee bed
\(A\) — puck cross-sectional area
\(\mu\) — dynamic viscosity of water
\(L\) — puck thickness
\(\Delta P\) — pressure difference across the puck

This equation shows that flow increases with pressure and permeability, but decreases when the puck is thicker or the water is more viscous.

05

Puck Permeability

The permeability \( k \) depends on particle size, packing density, and porosity. A common approximation is the Kozeny–Carman relation:

\[ k = \frac{\epsilon^3 \, d_p^2}{180\,(1-\epsilon)^2} \]
\(\epsilon\) — porosity of the coffee bed
\(d_p\) — average particle diameter

This shows that permeability scales approximately with particle diameter squared, \( k \propto d_p^2 \). Therefore, a finer grind greatly reduces permeability and slows flow.

06

Pressure & Flow Rate

A standard espresso machine operates near \( \Delta P \approx 9\;\text{bar} \), where \( 1\;\text{bar} = 10^5\;\text{Pa} \), so \( 9\;\text{bar} = 9 \times 10^5\;\text{Pa} \).

If the pressure is too low, water may pass through the puck slowly and under-extract the coffee. If the puck has cracks or uneven density, water may form channels, leading to uneven extraction.

07

Reynolds Number

The Reynolds number estimates whether flow is laminar or turbulent:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho\,v\,D}{\mu} \]
\(\rho\) — water density
\(v\) — flow velocity
\(D\) — characteristic length scale
\(\mu\) — dynamic viscosity

Inside the tiny pores of the coffee puck, the characteristic length scale is very small. This means the Reynolds number is usually low, so flow through the puck is mostly laminar.

08

Temperature Dependence of Extraction

Temperature affects both solubility and reaction/extraction rates. A simplified Arrhenius-type model can describe the temperature dependence:

\[ k_T = A\,e^{-E_a/(RT)} \]
\(k_T\) — temperature-dependent extraction rate constant
\(A\) — pre-exponential factor
\(E_a\) — activation energy
\(R\) — gas constant
\(T\) — absolute temperature (K)

Higher temperature generally increases extraction rate, but excessive temperature can produce bitter or harsh flavors.

09

Diffusion During Extraction

Soluble compounds inside coffee particles must diffuse into the surrounding water. A simple one-dimensional diffusion model is given by Fick's second law:

\[ \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D\,\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2} \]
\(C\) — concentration
\(D\) — diffusion coefficient
\(x\) — position
\(t\) — time

This equation describes how dissolved coffee compounds spread from regions of high concentration to low concentration.

10

Heat Transfer in the Coffee Puck

As hot water enters the coffee bed, heat transfers into the grounds and portafilter. A simplified heat equation is:

\[ \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha\,\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2}, \qquad \alpha = \frac{k_{\text{thermal}}}{\rho\,c_p} \]
\(\alpha\) — thermal diffusivity
\(k_{\text{thermal}}\) — thermal conductivity
\(\rho\) — density
\(c_p\) — specific heat capacity

Temperature stability matters because extraction rate and flavor balance depend strongly on water temperature.

11

Crema & Gas Formation

Crema is a foam made from carbon dioxide gas, oils, water, and coffee solids. Freshly roasted coffee contains trapped CO\(_2\), which is released during extraction.

The ideal gas law gives a simple model for gas behavior:

\[ PV = nRT \]
\(P\) — pressure
\(V\) — gas volume
\(n\) — number of moles
\(R\) — gas constant
\(T\) — temperature

When high-pressure espresso exits the portafilter and returns to atmospheric pressure, dissolved gases expand and contribute to crema formation.

12

Channeling & Uneven Extraction

Channeling occurs when water finds paths of lower resistance through the puck. In Darcy's law, this corresponds to local increases in permeability \( k \). If one region has higher permeability, then local flow increases:

\[ Q_{\text{local}} \propto k_{\text{local}} \]

This causes some regions of the puck to over-extract while others under-extract.

13

Conclusion

Espresso extraction is governed by a combination of fluid mechanics, heat transfer, diffusion, porous media physics, and chemistry. The most important variables include grind size, puck preparation, water temperature, pressure, flow rate, and extraction time.

A good espresso shot is not just a recipe — it is a controlled physics experiment.
